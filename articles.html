Democracy or Delusion?

By Rohan Vishwakarma

We are being told that democracy is the best form of government, it’s hard for me to admit this. We’re often reminded of Abraham Lincoln’s famous words: “Democracy is of the people, by the people, for the people.” But the older I get, the more I feel that democracy has become something else entirely. It feels like it’s for the loudest, by the largest, and of the most misled. When I look around—especially as an Indian—I see a system that rewards manipulation more than merit, marketing over morals. It’s a numbers game, where the most votes win, not the best ideas. And that’s not just a problem. That’s dangerous.

Rule of the Mass, not of the wise

Let’s talk honestly. In India, elections are no longer about finding the most capable leader. They’re about who can promise more. Free electricity, free water, free this, free that—leaders today are selling dreams they never intend to fulfil. And we the people are voting not for visionaries, but for campaign slogans. We’ve mistaken charm for competence. We’ve chosen the loudest speaker in the room and ignored the quiet ones who could actually lead us somewhere better. Think of it this way: if you were on an airplane, who would the passengers vote for the pilot? The who one promises everyone a luxury seat, but can’t even fly the plane, or the other who is a trained pilot but offers no false comforts? In democracy, the crowd often chooses the sweet-talker over the skilled professional. That’s exactly what we’ve done, time and again. We call this freedom. But what kind of freedom is it when our choices are built on illusions? True freedom isn't the right to pick your favourite toothpaste or choose from 20 brands of chips. True freedom is the ability to help shape the direction of your country—to be productive, to live with dignity, and to know that the system around you work for the truth, not just for the votes.

That comes late Is Injustice

One of the worst parts is our justice system. If you’ve ever had to fight a case in court, or known someone who has, you know this truth: justice in a democracy is often delayed, and justice delayed is justice denied. People wait years for their voices to be heard, while the powerful get swift judgments and endless protection. That’s not equality that was promised in democracy.

Leaders or Salesmen?

leaders have become salesman. They don’t need vision anymore—they need viral slogans. They don’t need to solve problems—they just need to look like they’re doing something. We elect them not for their ideas, but for their popularity and similarity. And that’s what democracy now produces: marketing experts. Not nation builders.

There a Better Way? Let’s Talk About It

Maybe you think I’m being too critical. But let’s just take a moment and look at other systems—not to adopt them blindly, but to learn what we’re missing. Communism, for example, has been depicted as an enemy in so many ways. But at its heart, it tries to do one thing right: “make sure no one is left behind”. It believes in equal opportunity, fair access to resources, and a life of dignity for all. Yes, it can become rigid and authoritarian. But at least it doesn’t treat healthcare or education as luxuries, like capitalism often does. Capitalism, on the other hand, gives you choice. It rewards innovation. But it’s cold. Ruthless. In capitalism, if you can’t run fast, you get crushed. People become numbers. Lives are measured in profit margins. The rich soar, the poor survive or don’t. And then there’s Ram Rajya—a system inspired by the idealized rule of Lord Rama. Let’s not get caught up in religion here. Ram Rajya isn’t about temples or rituals. It’s about morality in leadership. Imagine a country where leaders truly care, where justice is swift and fair, where no one is rejected, and where ruling is seen as a responsibility—not a way to get rich. Of course, Ram Rajya is a utopia. But even utopias can be useful if they help us aim higher.

So, What Do We Need?

I don’t think we need to throw out every system and start over. I think we need to be honest about what’s working and what’s not. We can learn from communism’s compassion, capitalism’s efficiency, democracy’s inclusiveness, and Ram Rajya’s morality. But what we absolutely do is stop pretending that democracy, just because it gives everyone a vote, is automatically just or fair. It’s not. Not when the vote can be bought. Not when the uninformed drown out the voices of the wise. Not when the f inal goal is winning, instead of serving.

Final Thoughts: Let’s Dare to Imagine Better

So, here’s my plea—not to tear democracy down, but to stop treating it like a sacred cow. Let’s question it. Let’s challenge it. Let’s improve it or replace it with something that works better. Shouldn’t we ask if a farmer in distress deserves the same attention as a billionaire with a failed startup? Shouldn’t we ask whether slogans and freebies are a good enough reason to trust someone with a nation’s future? Shouldn’t we stop calling this freedom when most of us are still trapped in poverty, broken systems, and endless waiting? I don’t want votes. I want vision and purpose. Maybe democracy was once our hope. But unless it changes, it’s becoming our delusion.
